Bed-Stuy sees most short-term rental applications under new city rules

Genna Contino

NEW YORK - After enforcement of a new local law took more than 70% of Airbnbs offline this September, new data is showing where legal short-term rentals remain across all five boroughs.

Airbnb and its hosts say prior to the new regulations, the platform was used as an alternative to pricey Midtown hotels, giving tourists a chance to explore the boroughs beyond Manhattan and support nearby small businesses.

The data show short-term rentals can still provide that for tourists – just on a smaller scale – with the highest number of registration applications coming from Bedford-Stuyvesant.

Airbnb global policy director Theo Yedinsky suggests some of the short-term rentals not appearing in the data are still available in an underground market.

“Short-term rental activity is moving underground forcing visitors to either pay exorbitant hotel prices or turn to unregulated black market accommodations, and travelers have fewer accommodation options in outer boroughs which means small businesses outside of Manhattan are cut off from the economic benefits of tourism,” Yedinsky said.
